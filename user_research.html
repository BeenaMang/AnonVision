Executive Summary

This research study examined the privacy protection needs and current workflows of content creators, journalists, educators, and researchers who regularly work with photographs containing identifiable individuals. Through semi-structured interviews and contextual inquiry with six participants at Iowa State University, we identified critical pain points in current face anonymization workflows and validated the need for AnonVision, a streamlined privacy protection tool.

Key Findings

Critical Pain Points Identified:

  • Extreme time consumption: participants spend 15-45 minutes per image using manual editing methods
  • Software barriers: 67% lack access to professional image editing software; those with access struggle with steep learning curves
  • Inconsistent results: manual processes lead to uneven privacy protection and missed faces
  • Privacy anxiety: all participants expressed concern about accidentally publishing unprotected identities
  • Batch processing limitations: no efficient way to process multiple images simultaneously

Primary User Needs:

  • Simplicity: 100% of participants want a solution "as easy as saving a file"
  • Speed: must process images in under 1 minute each
  • Preview capability: 83% need to see results before saving
  • Local processing: all 6 participants require privacy-preserving local processing without cloud uploads
  • Affordability: must be free or very low cost

Research Methodology

Study Design

This qualitative research study employed multiple methods to gain deep insights into user needs, behaviors, and pain points related to face anonymization in photographs. The study was conducted over two weeks in September 2025.

Participant Recruitment

Participants were recruited through purposive sampling from various academic departments. Recruitment criteria included:

  • Regular work with photographs containing identifiable individuals
  • Current or past need to protect identities in images
  • Diverse roles (students, faculty, staff) to capture varied perspectives
  • Different levels of technical proficiency

Participants were recruited via in person meeting within their departments

Research Methods

The study employed multiple qualitative research methods:

1. Semi-Structured Interviews (45 minutes each)

Individual interviews were conducted to explore participants' experiences, needs, and frustrations with face anonymization. The interview protocol included open-ended questions about current workflows, pain points, and ideal solutions.

2. Contextual Inquiry

Participants demonstrated their current anonymization process using their own tools and recent projects. This revealed workflow details, tool usage patterns, and hidden inefficiencies not typically captured through interviews alone.

3. Task Analysis

We documented step-by-step processes participants currently use, timing each stage to quantify efficiency issues.

4. Pain Point Mapping

Throughout interviews, we systematically identified and categorized frustrations, obstacles, and unmet needs.

Interview Protocol

The following questions guided our semi-structured interviews:

  1. Can you describe a recent situation where you needed to protect identities in photographs?
  2. Walk me through your current process for anonymizing faces, step by step.
  3. What tools do you currently use? What works well about them?
  4. What frustrates you most about your current approach?
  5. How do you decide what level of anonymization is appropriate for different contexts?
  6. How many images do you typically need to process at once?
  7. How much time does this process typically take you?
  8. Have you ever had concerns about privacy protection being inadequate?
  9. What would your ideal privacy protection tool look like?
  10. Are there features you wish existed but don't currently?

Data Analysis

Interview recordings were transcribed and analyzed using thematic analysis. We employed affinity diagramming to synthesize findings, grouping similar observations into themes. Journey mapping was used to visualize current workflows and identify opportunity areas for improvement.

Key Findings

Critical Pain Points

Through our research, five major pain points emerged consistently across all participants:

Pain Point 1: Extreme Time Consumption

All six participants reported spending between 15-45 minutes per image using their current manual methods. This time investment compounds dramatically with multiple images.

"Last observation period I took 150 photos. It took me three weeks of evenings just to process them all. That's not research time—that's just tedious manual labor." - P4

"I have to do each photo one by one. Last event I had 47 photos to edit. I spent my entire weekend on it." - P5

Impact: Time spent on manual editing directly reduces time available for core responsibilities (research, writing, teaching). Two participants (P2, P4) reported missing deadlines specifically due to photo processing delays.

Pain Point 2: Inconsistent Results and Quality Concerns

Manual processes led to uneven results, with participants expressing low confidence in the consistency and professionalism of their output.

"Sometimes I blur too much and lose context, other times not enough and you can still recognize people. There's no standard." - P2

"I feel like I'm back in kindergarten using Paint. It's embarrassing how unprofessional my photos look." - P1 (Emily)

Impact: Inconsistent anonymization creates legal/ethical risks. Unprofessional appearance damages credibility of published work. Participants often over-blur to compensate for uncertainty, losing valuable contextual information.

Pain Point 3: Software Access and Technical Barriers

67% of participants (4 of 6) lack access to professional image editing software. Those with access struggle with steep learning curves and complex interfaces.

"Our department can't afford Photoshop licenses for everyone. I'm left using whatever free tool I can find online." - P3

"I watched three YouTube tutorials and still can't figure out layers in GIMP. I just want to blur faces—why is this so complicated?" - P1

Impact: Software barriers force users toward inadequate solutions (Paint, PowerPoint) or create dependencies on limited computer lab access. Learning curves prevent adoption of better tools even when available.

Pain Point 4: Privacy Anxiety and Compliance Concerns

All participants expressed significant anxiety about potentially publishing images with inadequately protected identities. Academic participants face additional IRB and FERPA compliance requirements.

"I lie awake at night worrying I missed someone's face. One mistake could end my journalism career." - P2

"IRB requires documented proof that we've properly anonymized participants. With manual methods, how can I prove I didn't miss anyone?" - P3

Impact: Privacy anxiety causes stress and risk-averse behavior. Some participants avoid using photos entirely despite their value. Manual detection is error-prone, creating genuine compliance risks.

Pain Point 5: No Efficient Batch Processing

67% of participants regularly need to process multiple images but lack efficient batch processing capabilities.

"I have to open each photo individually, do the editing, save it with a new name, and move to the next one. With 40+ photos, it's soul-crushing repetition." - P5

Impact: Repetitive work causes physical fatigue (eye strain, repetitive stress). Batch needs go unmet, limiting use of photo documentation. Creates bottleneck in research and publication workflows.

Design Requirements

Based on our research findings, affinity analysis, and journey mapping, we have identified the following design requirements for AnonVision, prioritized using the MoSCoW method:

Must Have (P0) - Critical for MVP

Requirement Rationale Supporting Data
Automatic face detection Manual face selection is the primary cause of time consumption and errors. Automated detection addresses core pain point. 100% of participants manually select faces; average 3-5 min per photo just for selection
One-click processing Users demand simplicity equivalent to "opening and saving a file" with minimal configuration. 100% requested "one-click" or equivalent simplicity
Blur and pixelate options Different contexts require different anonymization styles. Blur preferred for general use, pixelate for stronger protection. 83% want choice between methods
Adjustable privacy levels Single privacy level insufficient for diverse use cases (classroom vs. protest, minors vs. adults). 100% need variable privacy strength; contexts vary significantly
Real-time preview Users need to verify appropriateness before saving; "see before you save" is critical for confidence. 83% require preview capability
Local processing only Research ethics, FERPA, and IRB compliance require no cloud uploads. Privacy is non-negotiable. 100% require local processing; several have institutional prohibitions on cloud use
Simple GUI interface Command-line interface would exclude primary user base; technical barriers already major pain point. 67% lack technical proficiency; all want GUI
Processing speed <60 sec/image Current 15-45 min/image is unsustainable. Sub-minute processing enables practical adoption. All participants cite time as critical factor

Should Have (P1) - Important for User Satisfaction

Requirement Rationale Supporting Data
Batch processing Users regularly process 20-150 images; individual processing still creates workflow bottleneck. 67% regularly process multiple images; P4 processes 150/session
Face count indicator Provides confidence that all faces detected; reduces anxiety about missed faces. 100% expressed anxiety about missing faces
Automatic file naming Prevents file management confusion; enables workflow efficiency. Best practice for batch operations
Common format support Users work with JPG, PNG, BMP from various cameras and sources. Multiple formats mentioned across participants
Visual detection boxes Shows which faces were detected; allows manual verification before processing. Reduces anxiety; increases confidence (83% value verification)
Progress indicator For batch operations; manages expectations and prevents perceived freezing. Standard UX best practice for operations >5 seconds

Could Have (P2) - Nice to Have Features

  • Comparison grid output: Shows all anonymization options side-by-side for evaluation
  • Drag-and-drop interface: Alternative to file dialog for improved UX
  • Undo functionality: Allows experimentation without consequences
  • Custom output directory: User control over save location
  • Processing history log: Documentation for compliance purposes (IRB)
  • Keyboard shortcuts: Power user efficiency improvements

Won't Have (P3) - Out of Scope for Initial Version

  • Video processing: Significantly more complex; focus on images first (noted in original spec as deferred)
  • Selective face protection: Ability to protect only specific individuals; adds UI complexity
  • Cloud storage integration: Contradicts privacy requirement
  • Advanced editing features: Would replicate Photoshop; contradicts simplicity goal
  • Mobile app version: Desktop focus for initial version

System Design Overview

Based on user requirements and technical constraints, the following system architecture has been designed:

Architecture Principles

  • Simplicity First: Minimize user configuration and decision-making
  • Privacy by Design: All processing local; no network dependencies
  • Performance: Target <60 seconds per image processing time
  • Reliability: Robust error handling; graceful degradation
  • Accessibility: Intuitive for non-technical users

Technology Stack

Component Technology Rationale
Face Detection OpenCV (Haar Cascade) Proven, fast, no network required. Backup: DNN-based detector for difficult cases
Image Processing NumPy + OpenCV Efficient array operations for blur/pixelation effects
GUI Framework Tkinter or PyQt Native Python; cross-platform; no additional dependencies
Programming Language Python 3.8+ Course requirement; excellent library ecosystem

Core Functional Modules

1. Image Input Module

  • File dialog for image selection (single or multiple)
  • Format validation (JPG, PNG, BMP)
  • Image loading and preprocessing
  • Thumbnail generation for batch operations

2. Face Detection Module

  • Automatic face detection using Haar Cascade classifier
  • Grayscale conversion for optimization
  • Multi-scale detection for various face sizes
  • Bounding box generation for detected faces
  • Face count reporting

3. Anonymization Module

  • Blur effect: Gaussian blur with adjustable kernel size
  • Pixelate effect: Block averaging with adjustable block size
  • Three strength levels: Light, Medium, Strong
  • Region extraction and processing
  • Processed region replacement in original image

4. Preview Module

  • Real-time preview window
  • Detection box overlay (green boxes)
  • Dynamic update on settings change
  • Zoom capability for detail inspection

5. Output Module

  • File save dialog
  • Automatic naming convention: [originalname]_protected.[ext]
  • Batch save capability
  • Success confirmation messaging
  • Optional comparison grid generation

Risk Mitigation Strategies

Risk: Inconsistent Face Detection Across Conditions

Description: Haar Cascade may struggle with profile views, occlusions, poor lighting, or non-frontal faces.

Impact: Users lose confidence; defeats core value proposition of automatic detection.

Mitigation:

  • Implement multi-angle Haar Cascade classifiers (frontal, profile)
  • Develop fallback to DNN-based detector for images with <3 faces detected
  • Add visual detection boxes so users can verify before saving
  • Include "Add Manual Detection" feature for missed faces (Phase 3)
  • Set clear expectations: "Works best with frontal faces in good lighting"

Risk: Performance Issues with Large Images or Batches

Description: High-resolution images or large batches may cause slow processing or memory issues.

Impact: User frustration; fails to meet <60 sec target.

Mitigation:

  • Implement intelligent downsampling for detection (process at lower res, apply at full res)
  • Use progress bars for batches to manage expectations
  • Test with maximum realistic image sizes (4K+ resolution, 50+ image batches)
  • Implement lazy loading for batch operations
  • Set reasonable limits with clear messaging (e.g., "Batch limit: 100 images")

Risk: User Expectations Exceed MVP Capabilities

Description: Users may expect video processing, selective anonymization, or advanced features.

Impact: Disappointment despite meeting stated requirements.

Mitigation:

  • Clear product positioning: "Simple, fast face blurring for images"
  • Explicit scope documentation: "Currently supports still images only"
  • Roadmap communication: "Video support planned for future version"
  • Focus user testing on stated capabilities
